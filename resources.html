About the Solar System:

Solar system, assemblage consisting of the Sun—an average star in the Milky Way Galaxy—and those bodies orbiting around it: 8 (formerly 9) planets with more than 210 known planetary satellites (moons); many asteroids, some with their own satellites; comets and other icy bodies; and vast reaches of highly tenuous gas and dust known as the interplanetary medium. The solar system is part of the "observable universe," the region of space that humans can actually or theoretically observe with the aid of technology. Unlike the observable universe, the universe is possibly infinite.

Moons:

  • Earth's Moon:

    • The brightest and largest object in our night sky, the Moon makes Earth a more live-able planet by moderating our home planet's wobble on its axis, leading to a relatively stable climate. It also causes tides, creating a rhythm that has guided humans for thousands of years. The Moon was likely formed after a Mars-sized body collided with Earth several billion years ago. Earth's only natural satellite is simply called "the Moon" because people didn't know other moons existed until Galileo Galilei discovered four moons orbiting Jupiter in 1610. In Latin, the Moon was called Luna, which is the main adjective for all things Moon-related: lunar.

  • Moons of Mars:

    • Mars has two moons, Phobos and Deimos. Both are believed to be captured asteroids or other debris from early in the formation of our solar system. Phobos is the larger of Mars' two moons. It orbits Mars three times a day, and is so close to the planet's surface that in some locations on Mars it cannot always be seen. Phobos is nearing Mars at a rate of six feet (1.8 meters) every hundred years. At that rate, it will either crash into Mars in 50 million years or break up into a ring. Deimos is the smaller moon. It orbits Mars every 30 hours.

  • Moons of Jupiter:

      • --Io: One of Jupiter's four largest moons known as the Galilean moons, stands out as one of the most extraordinary and geologically active bodies in our solar system. Io's surface is a mesmerizing blend of vibrant colors, primarily due to its extensive volcanic activity. It boasts the remarkable distinction of being the most volcanically active body in our solar system. Its surface is marred by hundreds of volcanoes, continuously spewing forth plumes of sulfur and other materials into space. These eruptions create a dynamic and ever-changing landscape, with lava lakes and extensive lava flows.
      -- Europa: Europa, one of Jupiter's remarkable moons, is a celestial body that has captured the attention and imagination of scientists and space enthusiasts alike. Europa is one of Jupiter's four largest Galilean moons, discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610. Europa is known for its icy surface, which conceals a global subsurface ocean beneath. Europa experiences tidal heating due to the gravitational forces exerted by Jupiter and its sister moons. This process generates heat within Europa's interior, keeping its subsurface ocean in a liquid state.

  • Moons of Saturn:

      • --Titan: Titan is the largest moon of Saturn and the second-largest moon in the entire solar system. It has a thick atmosphere primarily composed of nitrogen, with lakes and rivers of liquid methane and ethane on its surface. Titan has been a target of interest due to its Earth-like processes, making it a focus of study for potential prebiotic chemistry and even future human exploration.
      --Enceladus: Enceladus is a small moon but one of the most intriguing due to its geologic activity. It has a subsurface ocean beneath its icy crust, and plumes of water vapor and ice particles erupt from cracks in the surface. These plumes have been studied by spacecraft, revealing the presence of organic molecules and suggesting the potential for life.

  • Moons of Uranus:

      • --Miranda: It is the smallest of Uranus' major moons, with a diameter of about 290 miles (470 kilometers). Miranda is known for its chaotic and varied terrain, which includes deep canyons, towering cliffs, and large impact craters. The chaotic terrain on Miranda suggests a history of geological upheaval and resurfacing, possibly due to past tidal heating or geological activity.
      --Ariel: Ariel is one of Uranus' larger moons and is the fourth-largest of the planet's satellites. Ariel has a relatively smooth and lightly cratered surface, which indicates that it has been resurfaced by geological processes in the past. The moon displays a range of geological features, including valleys, fault scarps, and large, relatively young impact craters. Ariel's surface is composed of a mix of water ice and other materials, giving it a bright appearance.

  • Moons of Neptune:

      • --Triton: Triton is the seventh-largest moon in the solar system and the largest of Neptune's moons. It orbits Neptune in a retrograde direction, meaning it moves opposite to the planet's rotation. Triton's unusual orbit suggests that it was likely captured by Neptune's gravitational pull, rather than forming in orbit around the planet. Triton's surface is a mix of icy plains, ridges, and impact craters. Triton's surface is a mix of icy plains, ridges, and impact craters. The surface also exhibits geysers of nitrogen gas and dust, indicating that Triton is still geologically active. Triton has a thin atmosphere composed mainly of nitrogen, with traces of methane and other gases. Its atmosphere extends above the surface, and it results in a faint, pinkish hue due to interactions with solar ultraviolet radiation.
